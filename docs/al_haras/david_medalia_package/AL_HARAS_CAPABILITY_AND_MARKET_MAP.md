This document maps HYBA capabilities into Al Haras company offers, target markets, buyer personas, and evidence requirements.

## Buyer conversation architecture

### Conversation 1: Executive problem framing

Objective:

Establish the client's sovereign security problem.

Questions:

- Which AI or infrastructure systems are too sensitive to run as ordinary enterprise software?
- Where do you lack evidence of protected execution or output integrity?
- Which systems require national or board-level assurance?
- What cannot be outsourced to a foreign vendor or general-purpose cloud provider?

### Conversation 2: Technical diligence

Objective:

Show that HYBA has unusual substrate capability.

Assets:

- architecture map
- forensic review protocol
- protected execution demo
- tamper-evidence demo
- Themis/Al Haras boundary

### Conversation 3: Pilot design

Objective:

Scope a narrow pilot with clear evidence outputs.

Pilot should include:

- defined boundary
- defined data classification
- defined success criteria
- defined legal/export-control review
- defined evidence artefacts
- defined no-go constraints

## Pricing hypothesis

### Phase 1: Assessment and evidence pack

Fixed-fee engagement.

Range:

- strategic assessment: low six figures
- technical evidence pack: mid six figures
- classified-readiness or infrastructure-resilience review: high six figures

### Phase 2: Pilot

Milestone-based pilot.

Range:

- high six figures to low seven figures depending on data sensitivity, integration complexity, and legal/export-control review requirements.

### Phase 3: Platform licence / managed assurance

Recurring annual licence and support model.

Range:

- seven figures annually for sovereign or critical-infrastructure deployment.

## Go-to-market sequence

1. Build evidence room.
2. Validate legal and export-control boundaries.
3. Conduct advisor diligence with trusted experts.
4. Hold NDA-gated strategic conversations.
5. Convert one narrow pilot.
6. Use pilot evidence to build reference architecture.
7. Expand to platform licence.

## Non-negotiables

- no unsupported absolute security claims
- no governance/security confusion
- no weapons-optimisation positioning
- no customer-specific national-security adaptation before legal review
- no public claim without evidence
- no external demo without founder-approved claim boundary

## Strategic implication

Al Haras should not try to compete with every cybersecurity vendor. It should own a narrower, harder category: sovereign AI security assurance.

That category is where HYBA's technology stack appears most differentiated and where buyers have the highest need for mathematical assurance, protected execution, and trustable evidence.
